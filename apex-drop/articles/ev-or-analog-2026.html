Electric or Analog? Why 2026 Is Forcing Every Performance Brand to Pick a Side

APEX/DROP Newsroom

From the Range Rover EV to Porsche's 900-volt Mission X, the luxury and performance segments are splitting into two camps — and buyer appetite for EVs at the top of the market is far from settled.

Look across this year's drops and a split becomes obvious. On one side: Porsche's Mission X, a 900-volt EV hypercar successor to the 918. Land Rover's Range Rover EV, bringing zero-emission power to the top of the luxury SUV market. On the other: Bugatti's V16 Tourbillon, Lamborghini's turbocharged-but-still-combustion Temerario, and AMG holding the line with the CLE 63's traditional V8.

The tension isn't just technical, it's commercial. Reporting from earlier this year has flagged that luxury car buyers are moving away from EVs, not toward them, even as EV adoption climbs in the broader market. That puts cars like the Range Rover EV in an unusual position: technically impressive, but launching into a segment where the target buyer's actual appetite is uncertain.

Even committed EV-only manufacturers are hedging. Reporting suggests Rimac's leadership hasn't ruled out a gas-powered model despite the brand's electric-only history, and Lotus has reportedly opened the door to bringing combustion back into its lineup after disappointing EV sales. When the companies that bet earliest on electric-only hypercars start reconsidering, it says something about how unsettled buyer demand still is at this end of the market.

Ferrari's position is the one to watch longer-term. Its upcoming electric model — expected to be badged Elettrica — is deliberately not a supercar in the traditional sense, more a four-door coupe, and Ferrari has reportedly engineered a system to generate real exhaust-like sound from the electric powertrain. That's a tell: even the manufacturer building a genuinely electric flagship doesn't think it can sell one without addressing the emotional gap combustion loyalists say EVs haven't closed.

None of this means electrification is stalling at the top of the market — hybrid drivetrains are now standard across nearly every halo car on this list. But full electrification, without a combustion engine anywhere in the system, is still the minority position among 2026's most anticipated performance drops. Whether that changes probably depends less on horsepower figures than on whether buyers who've been resistant so far actually show up for cars like the Range Rover EV and Mission X once they reach showrooms.
